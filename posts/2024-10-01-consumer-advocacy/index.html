On Consumer Advocacy

September 29, 2024·2 mins Draft

It’s become necessary when making purchases to do a lot of research. Often, expertise in the subject is necessary to make a reasonable assessment possible. Sometimes, there is good advice available, but this is getting harder and harder to find. With major search engines accepting payola to inject ads into search results, major outlets disguising “native advertising” as neutral editorials, and the deluge of junky low-cost products, it’s getting harder than ever to know where to anchor knowledge.

Cars can be a great example of this. Legislation, regulation, and cultural change, have all changed the kinds of cars that are available. Automakers are disincentivized from making lighter cars via regulation, whereas heavier (and more expensive) trucks and SUVs are incentivized. The widespread adoption of these larger cars makes them safer, while making them more dangerous for everyone else, creating an arms race. Safety requirements continue to expand, requiring more computers and sensors wired throughout the vehicle. The “Cash for Clunkers” program took countless older, more affordable used cars off the road, driving up the prices that the market will bear. But don’t worry—the stealership dealership will generously help you finance to spread out the payments over 7 years at 12% interest, and pay an extra 50% of the purchase price in the process (which the dealership gets a cut of, naturally). Don’t worry: they won’t skimp on the add-ons.

Consumers are under pressure from every direction, and every industry.

The Battle Lines #

The lines of this conflict span the entire economy. Each industry attacks consumers a bit differently, but there are some broad areas where companies in various industries adopt a similar tact.
