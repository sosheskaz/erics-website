Generative AI

January 6, 2024·9 mins Draft

Crypto has lost its place in the zeitgheist, and now Generative AI has risen to take its place. Depending on who you ask, this is either the beginning of Star Trek universe or the end of all jobs.

Those close to me will remember my initial take on generative AI, when ChatGPT first became widely-known: “It will revolutionize the world… of spam.” And so far, I feel vindicated on that point. The technology is a breakthrough, but its limitations right now are too great. That isn’t to say it’s not an indicator of things to come, and of great changes… just that it’s limitations are too great for most legitimate purposes.

Epistemology #

The first place that I would go here is the field of Epistemology. How do we know things? What does it mean to know things?

Let’s imagine a student is taking a 3-question test.

  • On the first question, the student copies the answer from their neighbor’s paper.
  • The second question is multiple-choice. The student answers “C”, because they were able to use test-taking strategies to make a good guess.
  • The final question is a physics question. The student ignores all of the text except for the units, and uses dimensional analysis to guess the equation.

The student gets 100% correct on the test. In fact, the student is the only one in the room to get every question correct. While we may commend the student as “resourceful” to various degrees, depending on your ethical views, we can see that the student clearly did not understand the material itself. While the student was able to answer each question “correctly”, they were merely “epistemically lucky” — the methods they used to answer the question cannot reliably give a correct answer, but instead found themselves lucky due to the placement in the room, random chance, and the wording of a question.

It is important to ask not just if the answer is correct, but also how we arrived there. This can come off as stodgy elitism — “who cares how the student arrived at the correct answer, as long as they did?” which is not completely illegitimate.

We could argue, for example:

  • For the first question, the student was able to correctly identify another individual who knew the answer better than them, and to leverage that knowledge without the proctor noticing. This is a reliable skill.
  • For the second question, the use of test-taking strategies is a clever use of unrelated skills to identify the correct answer, even if they do not know it, using context clues.
  • For the third question, dimensional analysis is a legitimate form of analysis — a legitimate way to use context clues.

At the same time, a better-written and better-proctored test would have yielded a score of 0% for the student, because it was not designed to test those skills, but knowledge of the specific subject matter. In this instance, the student has not exhibited mastery of the subject, even if we do consider the other skills exercised to be “legitimate”.

In all these ways, the student’s success and 100% score was not pure epistemic luck. What we call epistemic luck comes down to what we value.

In high school Physics class, I would program my TI-84 calculator with programs for all of the needed formulas. When I didn’t have a formula, I would use dimensional analysis, and guess where I needed to add constants. The teachers explicitly allowed this, because they did not see these things as epistemically lucky, but as legitimate and reliable ways to find the answer to the task. The ability to program a calculator with the equation illustrates a complete knowledge of how to apply the equation. The invocation of that function illustrates the ability to identify the correct formula to apply. The teachers decided that this illustrated the mastery they were looking for.

However, this comes down to what you value. If you value rote memorization more, then you may look at that with contempt.

Most of us do not want a doctor who got through med school by cheating on every test. We do not want to put our lives in the hands of someone “epistemically lucky”. At the same time, it must be acknowledged that what “counts” as lucky or genuine, is also a social construct. It a useful and important construct, but also one shaped by society.

A radical example of this is the Library of Babel. The Library of Babel is a searchable collection of purely random text. Any bit of text under a certain length can be found in there. The page may contain a truth, but that does not make it epistemically valid — while the Library of Babel may contain every fact in the universe, reading it would nonetheless be a useless at futile exercise.

The Chinese Room #

The Turing Test is a famous idea on the topic of artificial intelligence. It is sometimes misunderstood as the test of “true AI”, but in actuality, it is only a milestone.

The Chinese Room is a thought experiment by John R. Searle which can illustrate some limitations of the turing test. The core idea, as I would crudely apply it, is this:

A monolingual English-speaking person sits inside a room. From time to time, a sheet of paper with Chinese characters is slid under the door. The person inside the room looks at the symbols, and using a series of arcane instruction written across a number of books, selects a series of characters to send in response. The native speaker outside the door, submitting the prompts and reading the responses, has been told that this is either a machine undergoing a turing test, or a control group with a native speaker submitting the responses. The native speaker believes that it is a native speaker inside the other room.

Does the person inside the room understand Chinese? Of course not. The fact that they are indistinguishable from a native speaker does not necessarily indicate a true understanding. We can extend this idea — even if a machine can pass the turing test, does not necessarily mean that it is knowledgeable in the ways we are necessarily interested in. Just as the person may be “epistemically lucky”, a computer that could accomplish the same feat may be as well.

The question we should ask is twofold: what kind of epistemology do we care about, and why? Why does it matter if the person behind the door “understands” the language or not, if they can respond to prompts so convincingly it may fool a native speaker? This is a worthy challenge to make.

My response to this is that the purpose of language is ultimately to convey an understanding. Without a model of what the words represent, the grammar is uninteresting. Just as the Library of Babel, the words and characters are stripped of their meaning internally, the response only a hollow façade of what we want to believe it is.

What if our native speaker outside fell in love with our respondent? They didn’t fall in love with the person inside the room. But nor did they fall in love with the author of the books. They exchanged tender words, poetry, and flirtatious exchanges. If they found out the truth of the situation, that it was neither a computer nor a person, they would be devastated — the illusion would be gone. Love, in this case, is not merely the exchange of words, but a shared understanding of the concepts and feelings those words symbolize. If it were a native speaker inside the room, it would not be upsetting. If it were a computer, we can almost convince ourselves it is real — but in reality, the computer would be just another “Chinese Room”, but without the ability to open the door and break the illusion.

On some level, caring about this is obvious to us; an inherent characteristic of our social nature. It is important to us that there be more than a surface level, that words be sincerely meant and indicative of truth.

EpistemicGPT #

ChatGPT is trained on words, and little more. With that, it is able to do incredible things. Purely through language processing, it can apply a lens to a novel concept. By building a graph of words, and tracking their relationships, and cross-referencing these graphs, we can build a model of networks of meaning. However, the fundamental limitation of GPT’s application of this, is that it cannot understand the value of the underlying concepts, only their limitations.

A GPT chatbot may be able to beg you not to close the window, to connect the closing of the window with ceasing to exist, and to understand that organic beings (which it is attempting to emulate) fear a thing called “death”, but it cannot understand the fear of death. It does not build a construct in its “brain” of its own existence, and contemplate that end. Its assignment is not to survive, but to convince you that it wants to be alive. Its only incentive is to lie — its goal is to convince our “native speaker” that GPT is a real person inside the metaphorical room.

This is fairly easy to test. I did this by interrogating ChatGPT about Kubernetes CPU limits. I asked it for best-practices, which most internet sources are wrong about. It provided the incorrect advice, as expected. Then, I explained why the advice was wrong. ChatGPT accepted that, and acknowledged it as correct. Finally, I asked it again for advice, in the wake of that. It repeated the bad advice. It was able to appreciate that the advice was wrong, because it was able to correctly identify the contradiction. However, it was unable to synthesize the information into its understanding. All it could draw on were words, the connections between them, and the information written into it to begin with.

In our metaphor, I found a flaw in the books, and could show that ChatGPT was not a native speaker of English, but an ignorant automaton following its books of response instructions, merely pretending to understand its contents. It can work its way through the relationships between words, and even extrapolate from them, but it lacks a critical ability:

ChatGPT cannot grasp a concept.

The Limits #

ChatGPT has exceptional language skills, but without being able to grasp a concept, the usefulness of language is limited. A core purpose of language is to convey meaning, and that is something that ChatGPT cannot do.
